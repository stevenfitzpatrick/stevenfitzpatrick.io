While working on a recent website and looking to improve page optimization/performance, I came came across a new plugin released by Google called Lighthouse . The Lighthouse tool will basically run a report on your website, and test your site for a whole list of web performance standards. Conveniently, it will also then let you know what your page is missing and what areas need improvement.

The main aim is to get your website to a Progressive Web Application or PWA level. If you don’t know anything about PWA’s, this is a good introductory link. Progressive Web Apps are built to be fast, feel native on mobile (you can also add PWA’s to your Android’s Homescreen) and work under any network condition.

You can run the Lighouse tools in several ways, straight from the command line, a plugin part of your build process (i.e. webpack) or maybe most convenienly as a Chrome Extension. With the Chrome Extension all you need to do is browse to page you want to check and click on the Lighthouse Icon (Just make sure you don’t have page open in multiple tabs). As soon as you do it, the extension will take over and run your page against a barrage of tests. If you don’t get a full green report the first time don’t worry, a lot of the items are quite new in web development and most sites out there would currently not pass this test.

So, I will try and help guide you on the path to a full 100/100 score on the Lighouse report. Starting with a few basic items that you will need that I bet your site doesn’t currently have.

Service Worker

Adding a Service Worker is key for any PWA, this is what will allows us to deliver a great user experience under any network condition — hello offline first. The Service Worker will allows you to control the caching strategy for your website, putting you back in control how your website should load it’s resources and requests. If you don’t know anything about Service Workers, you might be wondering how you actually add a Service Worker to your site.

The good news is that adding a Service Worker to your site is relatively easing, and there is great tooling out there to get help you get started.

  • sw-precache Node module to generate a service worker, produced by Google.
  • SW Precache Webpack Plugin Webpack wrapper around sw-precache (Used on this site) .
  • offline-plugin Another one for webpack users.
Manifest File

As I mentioned earlier, one of the main features of PWA is to get your web app have a native feel on mobile. Here is where the manifest file comes into play. Again very simple to set up, just a manifest.json to the root of your project and then link to the manifest file from your index.html. It is a simple JSON file, with a large list of possible configurations which let you control how your app would be displayed on mobile. Items such as the icons, launch screen, theme colour, short name for the app etc. Here is the list of minimum required settings for the Lighthouse test.

  • Contains start_url.
  • Links to icon images in 64×64 and 192×192 sizes.
  • Background & Theme color
  • Theme color
  • Name & Short Name for your app (max 12 characters).
  • Display property set to standalone.
What Else ?

The Service Worker and Manifest File are the two items that will really boost your score on the Lighthouse test, and which more and more websites are adapting each day. But there is another main feature required on your website, and actually a Service Worker won’t even work on your website without this (unless you are working on localhost).

Https, Https & Https

Absolutely the most important item of a PWA and the Lighthouse report is having your website https enabled. With all the power that comes with having a service worker controlling the responses to all your requests, https is required. Even aside from that for just plain security and best practice, if your website is not https enabled — don’t wait any longer and get that SSL Cert. Chrome will soon display a security display a warning in the browser for any non-https websites which have a password or credit card (duh) input on their page .

Ok really, what else ?

Lighthouse is constantly updating the requirements to pass the report, so it’s a good idea to check your website every few weeks even if you pass everything incase something new has popped up. Plus it’s a good regression test for any future releases. While I feel adding a Service Worker, Manifest and Https are the main wins to get your report green — there are lot’s more smaller items. Page Load Performace is huge, and you will be checked for the amount of resources you load and how you load them.

But I hope with the pointers outlined here you can get closer to getting a 100 on the Lighthouse report, and if not on your current site it can help get a good head start for your next project !
